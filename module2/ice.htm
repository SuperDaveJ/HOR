The mission of the Office of Emergency Planning, Preparedness, and Operations (OEPPO) is to provide and implement a comprehensive and professional Emergency Planning, Preparedness and Operations Program for the House of Representatives, ensuring the continuity of operations of the House and the safety of its Members, staff, employees, and visitors during emergencies.

Emergency Preparedness Training Program

OEPPO offers training in the following areas:

  • CPR/AED and Basic First Aid Training in coordination with OAP (monthly)
  • Escape Hood Training (monthly)
  • House Emergency Programs Overview Course (quarterly)
  • Guest Lectures or Special Event (annually)
  • OEC Roles and Responsibilities (monthly)
  • Member Office Briefings (as requested)
  • Web-based Training

Information

  • Bi-monthly newsletter

Exercises

  • Drills/exercise feedback through the newsletter

Please visit their Web site, http://oeppo.house.gov, for available training courses, online enrollment, and important tools for Office Emergency Coordinators (OEC).

Office Emergency Coordinators

Each office identifies a primary Office Emergency Coordinator (OEC) who is the focal point for emergency planning and preparedness for House offices. An alternate is also identified.

An OEC plays a critical role in preparing staff for emergencies that could arise at any time.  Active involvement in the House Emergency Preparedness Program could mean the difference between a calm, orderly response to an emergency and the confused, chaotic response often seen when people are unprepared to deal with a crisis. As a new employee to the House, you can take an active role in your office’s emergency preparedness efforts if you:

  • Know who your office emergency coordinator is.
  • Know the evacuation route from your office and other offices and/or buildings that you frequent.
  • Know where your office's primary and alternate Assembly Areas are located.
  • Know your Office Emergency Plan and any Continuity of Operations plans specific to your office.

Evacuations/Evacuation Drills

Evacuation is a common and highly effective protective action taken in response to a threat to an occupied building.  Evacuation involves moving personnel to a safe location away from a threatened building.

In the event of an evacuation, you will be directed by one or more of the following: building fire alarms, wireless emergency annunciator system, building public address system, or U.S. Capitol Police (USCP) officers.  You may receive follow-up notifications using a variety of communications media.  During an evacuation or evacuation drill, all office staff should move to the appropriate Assembly Area using defined evacuation routes.  OECs will then account for all office personnel and report status to the USCP. 

Staff not in their normal office areas at the time of an evacuation should evacuate according to the routes and procedures for the areas in which they are.

Mobility Impaired

One elevator in each building has been designated as a Primary Emergency Evacuation Elevator.  An alternate elevator has been designated in the event that the primary is not available.  Each of these elevators is marked with a blue mobility-impaired staging area sign.  Please locate these elevators in your building and contact your OEC or OEPPO for additional information.

Shelter-in-Place/Relocate

Shelter-in-Place means to take refuge inside, if leaving your office or building may put you in harm’s way.  Sheltering-in-Place is a way to keep you safe until help arrives or the incident is resolved.

A shelter can be an office, conference room, hallway, restroom, stairwell, or other location in which persons remain when an emergency warrants.  You should always follow the directions given by the USCP.

Escape Hoods

You may be asked to don (put on) protective equipment to ensure a safe breathing environment if there is a respiratory threat from a chemical or biological agent, or radiological particulate hazard.  All House offices have been issued the new escape hoods.  They are typically stored in your office in black rolling duffle bags with identification tags.  There are also many public caches throughout the Capitol Complex that contain escape hoods for Members, staff, and visitors.  Baby escape hoods are also available in the public caches.

It’s critical that you know:

  • How to don an escape hood
  • Where the escape hoods are stored in your office

Training is offered by the OEPPO.  A training video is available on their Web site at http://test.oeppo.house.gov/multimedia.aspx.  Minimal training is required, because of the ease of use of the escape hood.  Demonstrations are available upon request.
