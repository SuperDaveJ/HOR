The Office of Identification Services provides identification cards to permanent and temporary House of Representatives and Architect of the Capitol (AOC) staff, interns, pages, fellows, detailees, as well as the Members and the immediate family and spouses of Members of Congress.  Vendors and contractors sponsored by a House or AOC office are also issued identification badges.  In addition, ID Services issues liaison identification badges to paid employees of Executive Branch and other Congressional support agencies who have a specific, frequent business need to access the Capitol campus outside of public building hours.

Initial ID Badge

To obtain an ID card, new staff must follow this process:

  • Fill out a 110th Congress ID request form.
  • Obtain the signature of the employing Member (Officer) on the ID request form.
  • Submit the form to ID Services in person or by fax to 202-225-0239.  Forms should be submitted at least twenty-four (24) hours before an ID is required.  This enables data entry to be performed before the requesting staffer arrives at the ID Offices, and thereby reduces waiting time.
  • Presentation of proper photo identification, which includes a valid driver’s license, non-driver’s ID card, domestic or foreign passport, or U.S. military card, will be required before an ID badge is issued.

 

Lost Identification Cards

Lost ID cards must be reported to ID services, either by telephone or in person.  The reports should include the cardholder’s name, Social Security number, and fax number, so that an affidavit for a lost ID may be completed.  Both the Member and cardholder MUST sign the affidavit.  There is a waiting period of three (3) business days from the time the ID card is reported to ID Services as lost or stolen, before an ID will be reissued.

Temporary Identification Stickers

Permanent staff may get a temporary ID by reporting to the Capitol Police House Division Detail Office, located in B-220 Longworth House Office Building.  Please be prepared to present photo identification in order to receive a temporary ID.  In addition, your employment status will be verified prior to issuance of the temporary ID.

House Parking Program

Parking space allocations for each House office are determined by the Committee on House Administration.  In addition to House offices, employees of the Architect of the Capitol and U.S. Capitol Police are eligible to park in House permit-only areas.

Staff receiving a Metro subsidy are not eligible for parking permits—permanent or temporary.

How to Get a Parking Permit

Employees are authorized for parking permits by their employing office.  When an office parking administrator assigns a permit to an employee, notification of the assignment authorizing the employee and the vehicle to park is submitted on office letterhead, signed by the employing authority (Member or Officer), and sent to the Director, House Garages and Parking Security.  The notification should include the parking permit number from the office’s parking roster; the employee’s name; the make, model, and color of the vehicle; and the license plate information.  There is no charge for a non-reserved parking spot.

Staff wanting to reserve their indoor parking spaces should submit their requests in writing to the House Garages and Parking Security Office and include their current parking assignments and employee identification numbers.  This must be received by the 10th day of the month to be effective for that month’s payroll.  Similarly, to cancel a reserved parking space, House Garages and Parking Security Office must be notified by the 10th day of a month to be effective for that month.

U.S. Capitol Police

The United States Capitol Police provide comprehensive law enforcement services to the Congressional community and visiting public within the U.S. Capitol Building, Congressional Office Buildings, and adjoining streets and parks.  Officers conduct building and street patrols in order to deter and interdict criminal activity, and to respond to alarms and calls for service.  The U.S. Capitol Police also conduct investigations into criminal violations that occur within the Capitol complex.  In addition to law enforcement services, the U.S. Capitol Police are responsible for all security operations that affect the United States Congress and the Capitol complex.

  • Contact 911 in the event of any emergency (i.e., medical, fire, bomb threat, or threatening individual). For non-emergency police assistance, you may call 5-5151.

The House has developed a comprehensive information systems security program to protect its computer systems and data.  The Security Program includes automated tools that are constantly running on the network to deter hackers and other unauthorized users, as well as policies, procedures, and guidelines that our users follow to keep the network secure.  We recommend that you familiarize yourself with the security policies and guidelines, which can be found on HouseNet (see Information Security in the Technology section).

SecurIDs

SecurIDs allow users to access the House network from remote locations via dial-up or Virtual Private Network (VPN). Anyone who has a House ID and has permission to access the House network is eligible to have a SecurID.

How to Request a SecurID

  • Fill out a SecurID Request Form, found online on HouseNet (see Information Security in the Technology section) or by calling First Call+ at 202-225-8000—and fax it to the Information Systems Security Office at 202-225-0368.
  • If you work for a Member or Committee, the Member or Chief of Staff must sign the request. If you work for the CAO or any other House officer, the request must be signed by a supervisor.

 

How to Use a SecurID

  • Enter your User ID.
  • Enter your four-digit PIN followed by the number displayed on the SecurID. Be sure to enter all 10 digits in succession, without entering spaces between numbers.
  • Look at the left of the display window on the SecurID, and be certain that at least one hash mark appears before entering your PIN and the number shown on the token.  Remember that the number changes every 60 seconds.

 

If your SecurID has expired or is not functioning properly, please call the Information Systems Security Office at 202-226-4988.

Workers’ Compensation

The Federal Employees’ Compensation Act (FECA) is a law which provides compensation benefits to civilian employees of the United States for disability due to personal injury (including occupational disease) sustained in the performance of duty. Damage to or destruction of medical braces, artificial limbs, and other prosthetic devices incidental to a personal injury are also compensable. The FECA also provides for the payment of benefits to dependents if job-related injury or disease causes the employee’s death.  

Injuries on the Job

Please contact the Employee Services Generalist in the Office of Employee Services at 202-225-1435.  The Generalist will help you with the appropriate forms and with the filing of claims with the Department of Labor.  All forms and bills are to be submitted through the Office of Employee Services, and not directly to the Department of Labor.

Filing Claims

U.S. Department of Labor Form CA-1, “Federal Employee’s Notice of Traumatic Injury and Claim for Continuation of Pay/Compensation,” is the form to use when reporting a traumatic injury.

Labor Department Form CA-2, “Notice of Occupational Disease and Claim for Compensation,” is the form for reporting an occupational disease.  In addition, the employee should be given two copies of the appropriate checklist, Forms CA-35A through CA-35H, for the disease claimed (specific checklists have been devised for various conditions in order to facilitate submission of evidence).

Instructions attached to Forms CA-1 and CA-2 should be followed carefully. Form CA-1 should be filed within 30 days of the injury, and Form CA-2 should be filed within 30 days of the date the employee realized the disease or illness was caused or aggravated by his or her employment. The forms may be obtained from the employing agency or from the Labor Department’s Office of Workers’ Compensation Programs (OWCP).

Office Emergency Coordinators

Each office identifies a primary Office Emergency Coordinator (OEC) who is the focal point for emergency planning and preparedness for House offices. An alternate is also identified.

An OEC plays a critical role in preparing staff for emergencies that could arise at any time.  Active involvement in the House Emergency Preparedness Program could mean the difference between a calm, orderly response to an emergency and the confused, chaotic response often seen when people are unprepared to deal with a crisis.

  • Know who your office emergency coordinator is.
  • Know the evacuation route from your office and other offices and/or buildings that you frequent.
  • Know where your office primary and alternate Assembly Areas are located.

Evacuations/Evacuation Drills

Evacuation is a common and highly effective protective action taken in response to a threat to an occupied building.  Evacuation involves moving personnel to a safe location away from a threatened building.

In the event of an evacuation, you will be directed by one or more of the following: building fire alarms, wireless emergency annunciator system, building public address system, or U.S. Capitol Police (USCP) officers.  You may receive follow-up notifications using a variety of communications media.  During an evacuation or evacuation drill, all office staff should move to the appropriate Assembly Area using defined evacuation routes.  OECs will then account for all office personnel and report status to the USCP. 

Staff not in their normal office areas at the time of an evacuation should evacuate according to the routes and procedures for the areas in which they are.

Shelter-in-Place/Relocate

Shelter-in-Place means to take refuge inside, if leaving your office or building may put you in harm’s way.  Sheltering-in-Place is a way to keep you safe until help arrives or the incident is resolved.

A shelter can be an office, conference room, hallway, restroom, stairwell, or other location in which persons remain when an emergency warrants.  You should always follow the directions given by the USCP.

Escape Hoods

You may be asked to don (put on) protective equipment to ensure a safe breathing environment if there is a respiratory threat from a chemical or biological agent, or radiological particulate hazard.  All House offices have been issued the new escape hoods.  They are typically stored in your office in black rolling duffle bags with identification tags.  There are also many public caches throughout the Capitol Complex that contain escape hoods for Members, staff, and visitors.  Baby escape hoods are also available in the public caches.

It’s critical that you know:

  • How to don an escape hood
  • Where the escape hoods are stored in your office

Training is offered by the OEPPO.  A training video is available on their Web site at http://test.oeppo.house.gov/multimedia.aspx.  Minimal training is required, because of the ease of use of the escape hood.  Demonstrations are available upon request.
