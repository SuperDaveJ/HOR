House Leadership
Speaker of the House

The Speaker is elected by the House of Representatives, and is its highest-ranking officer (and the third most senior official in the entire Federal government).

The Speaker acts as leader of the House and wields broad-ranging powers, including:

  • Presiding over debate in the House
  • Making rulings on points of order
  • Having priority right of recognition on the Floor
  • Setting the agenda by deciding what legislation comes before the House
  • Appointing task forces and commissions
  • Overseeing the management of support functions to the House

Additionally, by modern practice, the Speaker serves as the primary Congressional spokesperson for his/her party.

 

 

Did you know the Speaker of the House is second in line to succeed the President?
The Presidential Succession Act of 1947 (3 U.S.C. § 19) establishes the order of succession to the office of President of the United States in the event that neither the President nor the Vice President is able to "discharge the powers and duties of the office."

As mandated by the Act, the order of succession following the Vice President is:
 
  • Speaker of the House
  • President pro tempore of the Senate
  • Secretary of State
  • Secretary of the Treasury
  • Secretary of Defense
  • Attorney General
  • Secretary of the Interior
  • Secretary of Agriculture
  • Secretary of Commerce
  • Secretary of Labor
  • Secretary of Health and Human Services
  • Secretary of Housing and Urban Development
  • Secretary of Transportation
  • Secretary of Energy
  • Secretary of Education
  • Secretary of Veterans Affairs
