After the Constitution
Three Branches of Government

The U.S. Constitution—specifically, the first three Articles—defines the three branches of the U.S. Federal government:

  • Legislative Branch
  • Executive Branch
  • Judiciary Branch
Through a system of separation of powers, or “checks and balances,” each of these branches has some authority to act on its own, some authority to regulate the other two, and some of its own authority, in turn, regulated by the other branches.

The Legislative Branch

The legislative branch—defined in Article I of the Constitution—consists of the Congress, which is comprised of two houses: the Senate and the House of Representatives.

The Constitution grants numerous powers to Congress, including the power to make Federal law, levy and collect taxes, declare war, establish post offices, and approve treaties. Congress also has the responsibility to monitor and influence aspects of the executive branch, which includes the powers of impeachment.

Each congressional house has particular exclusive powers-the Senate must give "advice and consent" to many important Presidential appointments, and the House of Representatives must introduce any bills for the purpose of raising revenue. However, the consent of both congressional houses is required to make any law.

The Constitution also includes the "necessary-and-proper clause", which grants Congress the power to "make all laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into execution the foregoing powers."

The Executive Branch

The executive branch—defined in Article II of the Constitution—consists of the President and his staff (including the Vice President and the Cabinet).

The Constitution grants several powers to the President, including the power to approve/disapprove bills, and, with Senate approval, make treaties and appoint several government officials. The President is also the Commander in Chief of the military, and is responsible for taking "care that the laws be faithfully executed."


The Judiciary Branch

The judicial branch—defined in Article III of the Constitution—consists of the Supreme Court and lower federal courts, whose judges are appointed by the President with Senate approval.

The Supreme Court deals with matters pertaining to the Federal Government, disputes between states, and the interpretation of the Constitution. Additionally, the Supreme Court can declare legislation or executive action made at any level of the government as unconstitutional, nullifying the law and creating precedent for future law and decisions. The Constitution gives the Federal judiciary the jurisdiction to reside over the following types of cases:

  • All cases affecting Ambassadors, other public Ministers and Consuls
  • All cases of admiralty and maritime jurisdiction
  • Controversies to which the United States shall be a Party
  • Controversies between two or more States
  • Controversies between Citizens of different States
  • Controversies between Citizens of the same State claiming lands under grants of different States
  • Controversies between a State, or the citizens thereof, and foreign states, citizens, or subject

Below the Supreme Court are the courts of appeals, and below them in turn are the district courts, which are the general trial courts for Federal law.
